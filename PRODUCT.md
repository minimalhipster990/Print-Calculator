# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Print Calculator is used by resin/MSLA print operators and small production labs that need to quote jobs, estimate material and machine costs, and schedule real batches during daily production work. Users are making operational decisions under time and margin pressure, often comparing printer capacity, resin use, fulfillment costs, deadlines, and queue feasibility.

## Product Purpose

The product exists to make resin print pricing and production planning credible enough for everyday use. It should help users understand the real cost of a print, set prices with deliberate margin and tax treatment, and organize queue output with assumptions that are visible, adjustable, and technically defensible.

Success means the interface feels trustworthy during repeated use: calculations are legible, settings are discoverable, assumptions are explicit, and the final numbers can be explained to a customer or production teammate without guesswork.

## Brand Personality

Reliable, serious, technical, precise.

The interface should feel clean and elegant while remaining dense enough for operational work. It should favor calm confidence over decoration, with a darker visual direction where contrast and readability stay strong.

## Anti-references

Avoid decorative SaaS dashboard tropes, excessive cards, vague marketing polish, heavy neon aesthetics, low-contrast dark mode, and visual noise that makes numbers harder to audit. The product should not feel like a landing page, a generic startup dashboard, or an ornamental design exercise.

## Design Principles

1. Make assumptions visible: if a number affects cost, capacity, timing, or price, the user should be able to see where it comes from.
2. Prefer auditability over drama: pricing and scheduling outputs must be easy to trace from inputs to final result.
3. Support repeated daily use: keep the interface compact, predictable, and calm for operators who revisit it often.
4. Separate concepts that behave differently: cost accounting, scheduler availability, tax, packaging, shipping, and margin should not be collapsed into ambiguous labels.
5. Use elegance as restraint: strong hierarchy, contrast, and spacing should clarify the tool rather than decorate it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast or better, especially for the dark visual direction. Avoid relying on color alone for warnings, status, or profitability cues. Keep labels explicit, numeric inputs scannable, focus states visible, and motion restrained for users working in focused production contexts.
